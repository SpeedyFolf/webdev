01. Lamprais

Banana Leaf Bundles of Joy: Unwrapping Lamprais


The food scene of Sri Lanka is quite unique because their culture has been intertwined with influences from Britain, Portugal, Netherlands and even Malaysia. From 1658 until 1796 Sri Lanka was by the Netherlands, during this period a ethnic group known as the Dutch Burghers settled in Sri Lanka. The Dutch Burgher's were decendents from Dutch, Portuguese and Sri Lankan ancestry and they introduced a dish that would later become lamprais.

The etymology of lamprais can be traced back to the Dutch word lomprijst or ‚Äúparcel of food‚Äù. This is essentially Sri Lankan comfort food at its best! Each time I eat lamprais I can feel my face start to glow with happiness and my soul light up with excitement while my tummy puffs up like a balloon from all the accompaniments in the dish. The cool thing about lamprais is that the dish is flexible and varies in the ingredients used depending on the family. The side dishes used whenever my mum makes lamprais include Seeni Sambol (caramelised onion relish) which has a spicy and sweet taste, Wambotu Moju (eggplant pickle), and a type of short grain rice called Samba cooked in ghee, Fish Cutlets (croquettes with tuna and potato filling) and Chicken Curry all wrapped up and steamed in banana leaves. The inclusion of the banana imparts a unique aroma and taste to the dish, adding a subtle earthy note.

02. Kottu

Kottu: When Leftovers Become a Symphony of Flavour

Kottu originated in the Tamil regions of Sri Lanka in the 1970s, likely in Trincomalee or Batticaloa. Throughout Sri Lanka, the distinctive sound of bench scrapers chopping and mixing roti with vegetables on metal cooktops is like music to the ears as a feast is about to arrive.


As a Sri Lankan, I can attest that this flavourful, inexpensive dish is arguably one of Sri Lanka's hidden treasures, one I find myself often craving. The humble origins of this dish highlight how ingenuity leads to delicious results. Kottu is a testament to how simple ingredients, when combined with creativity and skill, can create a meal that is both satisfying and full of character. It‚Äôs a dish that brings people together, whether enjoyed at a bustling street stall or made at home with family and friends.


The colour and vibrancy from the vegetables featured in the dish also make kottu look like a magnificent work of art that makes it both a treat for the eyes and your mouth.

  6. Add soy sauce, salt, and pepper to taste. Serve hot!

03. Egg Hoppers

Hoppers are Egglicious

Recipe Breakdown
Category Servings Prep Time Cook Time
Breakfast 5 - 6 3 - 4 hours 2 - 5 Minutes
Ingredients Method
  • 1 teaspoon dried yeast

  • 2 teaspoons sugar

  • 1/2 cup lukewarm water

  • Water from the coconut or plain water

  • Approx. 2 1/2 cups thick coconut milk
  1. Mix yeast and sugar in lukewarm water and leave aside 5 minutes or until it starts to froth. Put the rice flour into a large bowl. Make a well and add the yeast mixture and sufficient cold water or coconut water to make a soft dough. Cover and leave aside 3 - 4 hours to rise.

  2. When the dough doubles in size and 3 hours before required, add the thick coconut milk to make a thick yet pourable batter. Add salt to taste and leave aside. After 3 hours the batter is ready to use. It may be necessary to add a little more milk to get the correct consistency and with a little practice you will achieve perfection.

  3. To make hoppers, wipe the pan with a piece of cloth dipped in oil. When the pan is hot pour a ladle of the batter. Swirl the pan around so that the batter covers the sides, be careful not to spill the batter. Cover and cook on a low heat until the top edge begins to turn golden. Remove hopper with a metal spatula. Wipe pan again with the oiled cloth and repeat.

  4. For egg hoppers, pour a ladle of batter into the pan. Then break an egg into the centre. Cover and cook on a very low heat until the egg is just done.

  5. Serve immediately with your choice of sambols, curries or chutneys for a traditional Sri Lankan breakfast experience.
